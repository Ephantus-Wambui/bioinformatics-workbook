## A


<span style="color: #518cc2; font-weight:bold">Alignment</span> [Sequence Alignment] -
<span style="color: #24376b;">
in Bioinformatics, means <b>finding matching fragments in compared sequences</b> of biopolymers (DNA, RNA, proteins, peptides). Each sequence is represented as a string of letters corresponding to the monomer types (4-letter alphabet for nucleic acids and 20-letter alphabet for polypeptides), following their order in the molecule. All sequences aligned must be converted to the same 1-letter notation (either nucleotide or amino acid). The sequences are written horizontally in rows, and empty positions (gaps) are inserted between the letters so that the following columns contain identical or similar characters. The final pattern of matching data positions is called a sequence alignment. <br>
The variations of sequence alignment facilitate many bioinformatics tasks, such as: <br>
• [<i>in proteomics</i>] identification of functional, structural, or evolutionary relationships between proteins based on the similarity of the sequences [see <b>Multiple Sequence Alignment</b>] <br>
• [<i>in genetics</i>] investigating the single-nucleotide polymorphism at a specific position in the genome or population [see <b>MSA for SNP</b>] <br>
• [<i>in transcriptomics</i>] investigating gene expression based on the sequenced RNA [see <b>Transcriptome Assembly</b>] <br>
• [<i>in transcriptomics</i>] identyfication of genes location in the reference genome based on the sequenced RNA [see <b>Genome-guided Assembly</b>] <br>
• [<i>in genomics</i>] reconstructing the genome based on the sequenced DNA fragments [see <b>Genome Assembly</b>]
</span><br>

<span style="color: #518cc2; font-weight:bold">Alternative Splicing</span> [Differential RNA splicing] -
<span style="color: #24376b;">
is the process (* <i>natural in vivo in eukaryotes</i>) of assembling various messenger RNAs (<b>mRNA</b>) from a pool of <b>exons</b> encoded by a single DNA gene. That allows significant compression of genetic material because a single gene code for multiple products, such as protein variants (<b>isoforms</b>) and functional non-coding RNA. The most common mechanism for creating truncated mRNA alternatives is (selected) <b>exon(s) skipping</b>.
</span><br>

## R

<span style="color: #518cc2; font-weight:bold">Read</span> -
<span style="color: #24376b;">
is a text in 1-letter per nucleotide base notation for a single fragment of a sequence [DNA/RNA] obtained from sequencing experiment. Due to the huge size of genetic material (~250 million nucleotide in a single chromosome), a typical sequencing experiment requires fragmentation. The superset of selected/filtered fragments of unknown sequences creates the library. During sequencing, library items are encoded, providing a bulk set of reads (i.e., text strings specifying the order of nucleotides in fragments).
</span><br>
